5 Course Objectives and Learning Goals

In this course, you will continue your journey in mastering accounting. Accounting is often called the language of business. To truly master a new language, one must practice it within the specific scenarios where it is spoken—just as learning vocabulary for dining requires engaging in the setting of a restaurant. Similarly, to learn the language of business, you must immerse yourself in the background of business itself. This course will therefore delve into the real-world business activities, practices, and transactions that financial accounting describes. We will explore the key concepts, theories, and mechanics of financial accounting, with a focus on the financial accounting of publicly traded firms, building on what you have learned in ACCT 321.

5.1 Topics

  • Compared to ACCT 321, this course covers topics that remain common across most businesses but involve greater complexity.
  • These topics will challenge your understanding of fundamental financial statement elements—assets, liabilities, equity, revenues, expenses, gains, and losses.
  • As we progress, you will see that even complex transactions can often be broken down into simpler components. For instance, lease contracts share similarities with purchases made through installment notes.
  • Additionally, we will explore more areas governed by detailed GAAP regulations rather than intuitive accounting logic. While these rules may initially seem arbitrary, we will examine the reasoning behind them by considering the dynamics of accounting standard-setting.
  • Two touchstone topics in this course are accounting for income taxes and the statement of cash flows:
    • Accounting for income taxes is among the most error-prone areas for public companies, representing more than 25% of all SEC comment letters.
    • The statement of cash flows requires a comprehensive understanding of cash flow implications for nearly every transaction we cover, as well as a solid grasp of the relation between cash accounting and accrual accounting.
    • I take pride in my students’ strong mastery of these topics compared to many other accountants.

5.2 Connections to Other Courses and CPA Exam

  • This course builds upon the knowledge you acquired in ACCT 201 and ACCT 321. A weak foundation could lead to negative consequences and hinder your learning experience in this class. Unfortunately, I cannot provide comprehensive re-education of all the material you should be familiar with at this stage. To help you identify areas for improvement, a self-diagnose test will be provided. If you find that a prerequisite concept is not well understood, I strongly recommend seeking tutoring at any point.
  • You will have the opportunity to revisit the topics covered in this class in ACCT 408: International Financial Reporting & Analysis, from an IFRS perspective rather than US GAAP.
  • Your understanding of key concepts such as the entity assumption, the distinction between net income and other comprehensive income, and the basics of financial derivatives will be crucial for your success in ACCT 420: Advanced Financial Reporting and Analysis.
  • All the topics covered in this class will be tested in the Financial Accounting and Reporting (FAR) section of the Certified Public Accountant (CPA) exam. Some technical accounting and reporting topics, economic analyses I demonstrate, and financial decision-making will also be part of the Business Analysis and Reporting (BAR) section of the CPA exam.
  • Although the topics covered in this class are relevant to CPA exams, it’s important to note that it’s not an alternative to your exam preparation courses. The fact that you will be encountering these topics for the first time makes it challenging to achieve CPA-level proficiency within the time allotted for a single semester. However, the study materials provided in this class are comprehensive and even exceed the requirements of CPA exams. It’s a vast repository of knowledge that ambitious students can explore. CPA exam preparation classes are typically offered in most master of accountancy programs. For instance, the MSA program at the University of Akron provides two Professional Colloquium courses that cover the Financial Accounting Reporting (FAR) and Auditing and Attestation (AUD) sessions.

5.3 Professionalism

Success in this course depends heavily on the active effort and dedication you invest beyond class hours. As future business leaders, you are expected to demonstrate professional manners and diligence.

To maintain a productive learning environment:

  • Classroom Decorum: Please limit the use of electronic devices to class-related activities and minimize irrelevant chatter. To facilitate an engaging environment, the instructor reserves the right to assign seats.
  • Active Engagement: Listening, observing, participating, and writing are keys to internalizing the material. During each class, we will solve practice questions together, and you are responsible for taking proper notes. Experience shows that students who attend and actively engage in lectures achieve significantly better grades.
  • Course Policies: You must adhere strictly to all course policies, attend classes regularly, and complete all out-of-class assignments and readings with care and integrity.

Please note that students with limited exposure to or interest in the technical aspects of financial accounting will find the depth of this course highly challenging. Diligent preparation and professional commitment are necessary to successfully navigate this material.

5.4 AI Proficiency

As the world embraces the Generative AI (GenAI) era, this class explores the potential of GenAI to enhance the capabilities of accountants. While GenAI can assist accountants in various tasks, it cannot replace the importance of in-person interactions in client relations and critical thinking. This course goes beyond mere bookkeeping; it prepares you for long-term career success by emphasizing critical thinking through in-class discussions. By the end of the course, you’ll gain a comprehensive understanding of fundamental accounting principles used in external financial reporting, learn how to effectively utilize AI tools, and develop your critical thinking skills and leadership abilities.

5.5 Coverage of International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)

Each chapter in the text makes appropriate references to IFRS and its relevance to the chapter material. You may review the green-colored IFRS boxes in each chapter. While US GAAP will be the basis for our discussions of financial reporting in this class, knowledge of IFRS is necessary for professional accounting competency. It is important to be aware of the concepts surrounding IFRS, and the relevant differences between US GAAP and IFRS. In-depth discussions on IFRS are covered in ACCT 408.

9.1 Important Notes on Letter Grade Assignment

Meeting the minimum point thresholds listed in Table 2 is a necessary condition but does not guarantee the corresponding letter grade. The following additional rules apply:

  1. Exam Performance Caps: To ensure core competency, obtaining a score below 60% on any of the three exams is considered failing that exam. If you fail one or more exams, your final course letter grade will be restricted (capped) as follows, regardless of how many total points you accumulate in the course:
    • Failing 1 exam (scoring < 120 points on Midterm 1 or 2, or < 168 points on the Final Exam): Your final letter grade is capped at a maximum of B.
    • Failing 2 exams: Your final letter grade is capped at a maximum of C.
    • Failing 3 exams: Your final letter grade is capped at a maximum of F (automatic failure of the course).
  2. ‘A’ Grade Cap & Allocation: The maximum number of straight ‘A’ grades awarded in this course is capped at 25% of the total registered student enrollment plus 4, rounded down (e.g., if there are 30 enrolled students, \(30 \times 25\% + 4 = 11.5 \rightarrow 11\) straight ‘A’s maximum). Since earning \(\ge 920\) points does not guarantee a straight ’A’, the available ‘A’ slots will be allocated using the following priority hierarchy (within each tier, students are ranked based on their total points):
    • Priority Tier 1 (Qualitative Excellence + Point Excellence): Slots are first allocated to students who already meet the point threshold for a straight ‘A’ (\(\ge 920\) points), have never failed an exam, and also receive a discretionary grade bump.
    • Priority Tier 2 (Qualitative Grade Bumps to ‘A’): Next, slots are allocated to students who earned an ‘A-’ through points (\(900\) to \(919.9\) points) and receive a discretionary grade bump that raises them to a straight ‘A’.
    • Priority Tier 3 (Points Ranking / No Grade Bump): Next, slots are allocated to students who met the point threshold for a straight ‘A’ (\(\ge 920\) points) and have never failed an exam, but did not receive any grade bump, provided they maintained satisfactory class participation.
    • Priority Tier 4 (Poor Participation / Lowest Priority): Lastly, slots are allocated to students who met the point threshold for a straight ‘A’ (\(\ge 920\) points) and have never failed an exam, but had poor class participation. These students are the most likely to be restricted to an A- if the straight ‘A’ cap is exceeded.
    • Note: Students who meet the points requirement for an ‘A’ (\(\ge 920\) points) but are not allocated a straight ‘A’ due to the cap will receive an A-.

Discretionary Grade Bumps: At the professor’s discretion, students may receive a grade bump (e.g., moving up one grade step, such as B to B+ or D- to D) to reward outstanding qualitative performance. This includes:

  • Exceeding expectations on challenging assignments.
  • Expressing consistently insightful ideas during class discussions.
  • Making notable contributions that facilitate the learning experience for everyone.

Important constraints on Grade Bumps: 1. Exam Cap Override: A grade bump cannot bypass the grade limits established by the Exam Performance Caps (e.g., if a student fails one exam, a grade bump cannot raise their grade past the B maximum cap). 2. ‘A’ Grade Cap Interaction: If a discretionary grade bump raises a student’s grade from an A- to a straight A, this will occupy one of the available straight ‘A’ slots under the 25% + 4 cap.

9.2 Explanation of Grading Items

  • You will be informed of your progress to 1,000 points at any time in the gradebook.
  • Active participation in class is crucial for enhancing the learning experience. A lack of voluntary discussion will negatively impact your letter grade. To facilitate participation, please bring a name tent to each class.

Each Pre-Class Assignment is worth 5 points. The seven highest scores of the eight Pre-Class Assignments will be counted into your gradebook, and the lowest grade will be dropped.
  • Post-Class Assignments: For each chapter, you will complete a Post-Class Assignment. Each Post-Class Assignment is worth 15 points and is open book. These assignments have unlimited attempts, and I will take the highest score. Solutions are provided after the due date, and I will not answer specific questions from the assignment until after the due date. The seven highest scores of the eight Post-Class Assignments will be counted into your gradebook, and the lowest grade will be dropped.
  • AI Accounting Analytics: As mentioned in the “Generative AI (GenAI) Policy” section, there are seven AI Accounting Analytics assignments in total, each worth 30 points. To provide flexibility for unforeseen circumstances, your lowest score among these seven assignments will be dropped when calculating your final grade.
  • Midterm exams: Two 1.5-hour-long midterm exams will be given during the term, each worth 200 points. Additionally, a two-hour-long comprehensive final exam will be given at the end of the term, worth 280 points.
  • Exam rules:
    • All exams are closed book. You are NOT allowed to use the internet to search for answers or discuss the exam with others.
    • Take-home exams: You must log into Connect® to complete the exams. Make-up exams are not offered, and a missed examination will result in a score of zero, unless excused by the instructor in advance.
    • Exam availability: Exams will be available on exam days from 8:00 AM to 11:59 PM. You may start the exam at any time during these hours but must complete it by 11:59 PM. Submissions after 11:59 PM will be subject to the hourly late submission penalty.
    • Proctoring: Proctoring functions are enabled for exams. If you have concerns about the recording function, please inform the instructor in advance, and an alternative in-person exam may be arranged.
    • A practice exam will be provided before each exam. All practice exams are due two days before the actual exam to allow time for using the study mode, which enables you to practice using different sets of numbers for supported questions. A small amount of bonus points is attached to each practice exam if you complete it early.
    • Exam Content: The exam questions are drawn from a limited pool of our in-class exercises, post-class assignments, practice exams, and previous exams conducted during this session.
  • Bonus points: You will have several opportunities to earn bonus points:
    • A small amount of bonus points is attached to each practice exam if you complete it early (based on the number of questions completed).
    • Bonus assignments may be assigned during the semester. These assignments require you to go beyond the classroom to investigate firms’ real-life disclosures. They aim to connect our class discussions with practical applications. The points assigned to each bonus assignment vary. However, completing a bonus assignment is not a viable solution to compensate for unsatisfactory performance in exams and routine assignments.

9.3 Late Submission and Make-Up Policy

These policies apply across the board to all assignments and evaluations (including exams) in this course:

  • Due Date Responsibility: You are responsible for monitoring the due dates for all assignments and exams on the course calendar.
  • No Make-ups or Individual Extensions: There are NO make-ups for graded assignments or exams, and the instructor does not offer individual extensions for any due dates.
  • Late Submission Penalty: If an assignment or exam due date has passed, you can still submit it. However, your grade will be discounted for late submission by 1% for every hour that passes after the due time. Consequently, you will lose all points for the assignment or exam after 100 hours.
  • Dropped Grade Policy: The purpose of dropping the lowest score(s) on routine assignments (e.g., Pre-Class and Post-Class Assignments) is to accommodate unanticipated emergency situations, personal illnesses, or schedule conflicts that you may encounter during the semester. Additional accommodations should adhere to the guidelines provided by the accessibility office.

10 Generative AI (GenAI) Policy

  • GenAI is revolutionizing the world at an unprecedented pace. AI tools are incredibly powerful in enhancing the learning process and automating repetitive or mechanical tasks. Regardless of individual opinions on AI, it’s undeniable that employers seek talent who can effectively utilize AI to boost productivity.
  • In this class, you will not only use AI as your tutor but also gain practical experience in how AI can assist accounting professionals in researching accounting policies and extracting financial information from publicly traded companies through a series of AI and accounting analytics assignments.
  • These assignments will also encourage you to reflect on the limitations of GenAI, the potential harm of AI slop, and its impact on human cognitive abilities.
  • To prevent you from outsourcing all your work to AI, your response to the last question of each AI assignment must be handwritten and submitted in person within a week after the assignment’s due date. Failure to comply will result in a zero for the assignment.
  • The inappropriate or unethical use of such technologies will violate the Code of Student Conduct, which includes cheating, plagiarism, fabrication, unauthorized collaboration, misrepresentation, and gaining an unfair advantage. For instance, it is not permitted to use any GenAI assistance during exams.

11 Tips for Success

You will succeed in this class by carefully following the instructions and policies. However, intrinsic motivation will benefit you in the long run. There are numerous books and people you can seek advice from; therefore, I will cover what I deem most essential below:

  1. Your identity will shape the approaches you take and the outcomes you achieve. Clearly identify who you are or who you want to be, and find a strong connection between that vision and the subject you are learning (in our case, financial accounting). However, setting an identity that is too difficult to achieve may harm your mental wellness.
  2. Be monomaniacal about the things that align with your identity. Monomania is defined as “a condition in which someone is extremely interested in one thing, in a way that is not normal” (Cambridge Dictionary). I argue that it can be a perfectly normal state where you enjoy the “flow state” and achieve professionalism.
  3. Do not be afraid of failing when seeking help. Steve Jobs testified to this point (watch it here). If you know a strong classmate or friend who can help you understand financial accounting better than I can, seek their help and return the favor by helping others. If I do not reply to you quickly enough, reach out again and remind me. Nothing will happen if you sit back and do not ask at all.
  4. Additionally, it is important to note that the contents of this class are interconnected through the fundamental accounting equation (i.e., Assets = Liabilities + Equity; also known as the ALE equation). Therefore, please find answers to your questions before we move on to the next topics to prevent confusion from snowballing.
