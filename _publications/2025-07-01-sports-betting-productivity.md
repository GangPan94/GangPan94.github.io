**Authors:** Zhiru Lin (DePauw University, [zhirulin@depauw.edu](mailto:zhirulin@depauw.edu)), **Gang (Ernest) Pan** (The University of Akron, [gpan@uakron.edu](mailto:gpan@uakron.edu)), Mengmeng Wang (The University of North Carolina at Greensboro, [m_wang7@uncg.edu](mailto:m_wang7@uncg.edu))

**Abstract:** Exploiting the staggered launch of legal sports betting across U.S. states, we find that employee productivity declines following the launch. The effect is stronger among firms whose work relies more heavily on cognitive effort and employee skill, consistent with an attention-allocation mechanism: expanded access to sports betting increases at-work distraction, diverting cognitive resources away from productive tasks. We also find that firm hiring and attrition both rise following the launch, increasing employee churn, and R&D and capital expenditures increase as well, with some evidence that this investment response, though not the hiring pattern, is driven by the productivity decline. Our findings show that the costs of speculative market liberalization extend beyond the bettors themselves: firms bear an economically significant productivity cost through their workers' attention and respond by adjusting labor and investment inputs.
